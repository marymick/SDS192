Course Description & Objectives

  • Official course description: On Smith College Course Search.
  • Objectives: This semester you will
    1. Take your first steps into the world of data.
    2. For those new to programming, learn just enough coding and computational thinking to work with data. For those with some experience programming, start expanding your “data science toolbox.”
    3. Dip your toes into the world of open-source software and start engaging with the open-source community.
    4. Tell your first “data stories” via projects.

Topic Schedule and Readings

A rough topic schedule and corresponding readings are posted below and on the main page. We will draw from the following sources:

  1. “ModernDive: An Introduction to Statistical and Data Sciences via R (MD)” by Ismay and Kim (link in menubar above).
  2. “Modern Data Science with R (MDSR)” by Baumer, Kaplan, and Horton.
  3. “Text Mining with R (TMR)” by Silge and Robinson.
  4. DataCamp: an online interactive environment for learning data science currently via R and Python. On top of the DataCamp courses we’ll cover this semester, you have free access to all their courses for 6 months.

Evaluation

Problem Sets 30%

Problem sets will involve computational assignments with written explanations. All homework must be submitted electronically before midnight of the due date. While no late submissions will be accepted, the lowest two problem set scores will be dropped in the final problem set grade.

More details coming soon.

Quizzes and Labs 20%

This course will include quizzes and lab sessions. Advance notice will be given for all quizzes. The labs will be conducted primarily in R and/or DataCamp, with some supplemental exercises in other platforms at the my discretion. Students are strongly encouraged to work in groups during these labs.

Projects 40%

You will work on 4 group projects over the course of the semester. These projects will be structured, but fairly open-ended to allow you be creative. Evaluation will emphasize originality and ingenuity in addition to sophistication and complexity.

  1. Mini-project 1 on data visualization (worth 5%) due Friday 10/5 5pm
  2. Mini-project 2 on data wrangling (worth 7.5%) due Friday 11/2 5pm
  3. Mini-project 3 on geospatial data (worth 12.5%) due Friday 11/30 5pm
  4. Final project (worth 15%) due Friday 12/21 (last day of finals) 5pm

Engagement 10%

It is difficult to explicit codify what constitutes “an engaged student,” so instead I present the following rough principle I will follow: you’ll only get out of this class as much as you put in. Some examples of behavior counter to this principle:

  • Not participating in class.
  • Engaging so little with me, either in class or during office hours, that I don’t know what your voice sounds like.
  • Shirking group work.
  • Ghosting.
  • Doing the bare minimum to get by.

Expectations

  • DataCamp:
    1. DataCamp is meant to be low stakes-practice, so the only thing that matters for your grade is whether you complete the course. So while things like the number of hints/solutions taken don’t factor into your grade, it is important to make a good faith effort to answer these questions the best you can.
    2. Taking notes based on what you cover is not required, but please do so if you feel you will benefit.
    3. You should be taking no more than roughly 4 hours per DataCamp assignment.
    4. Please note retention of the material will not be perfect the first time through. The only way to really retain the material is via lots of practice/doing. For now, think of this as a introduction.
    5. You do not need to complete any DataCamp courses in preparation for future lectures; they will always be explicitly assigned and you will subsequently be given time during Tu/Th lab sections to do them.
  • R components
    1. Collaboration: While I encourage you to work with your peers for problem sets and labs, you must submit your own answers and not simple rewordings of another’s work. Furthermore, all collaborations must be explicitly acknowledged in your submissions.
    2. Honor Code: All your work must follow the Smith College Academic Honor Code Statement; in particular all external sources must be cited in your submissions.
